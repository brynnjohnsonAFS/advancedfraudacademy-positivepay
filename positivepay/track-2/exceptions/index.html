Exceptions: the screen Positive Pay lives or dies on.

Start with the dashboard.

Most treasury teams open Positive Pay in the morning and land on the home dashboard. The view splits three ways: combined, check-only, ACH-only. Each shows exception count, dollar volume, decision mix, and the top reasons fraud is getting flagged.

This is the screen that turns "how's Positive Pay going" into a one-look answer. But the dashboard is the summary. The work happens deeper.

The Exceptions queue.

The queue is the screen that determines whether your program works. A check or ACH came in, it didn't match the issued file or the authorization, and now somebody has to make a pay-or-return decision before cutoff.

If exceptions are buried, missed, or routed to the wrong person, fraud gets paid. If they're easy to find and easy to decision, fraud gets stopped. There is no middle ground.

Anatomy of an exception.

Click into a single exception and you see exactly what the system flagged and why. Mismatched fields are highlighted in red, with the specific reason called out.

The cleaner this screen is, the higher your Decision Rate metric (Lesson 2.1). Business clients decision their own exceptions when the call is obvious. They default to your team when it isn't.

The system also catches relationships humans would miss — like a duplicate presented check using the same number.

The matching logic, visualized.

Every exception is a comparison between the issued check the business client uploaded and the presented check that hit the account. You can pull up both sides at once.

The system catches what humans miss.

Here's a feature most FIs don't know exists until we show them: the Default Decision Log.

If a business client doesn't decision an exception by cutoff, the system applies the configured default (pay or return) and logs it. Every default is captured so you can show your board exactly what happened, when, and to which exceptions.

This matters for two reasons. First, it means exceptions never just disappear. Second, it gives you a clean audit trail when a client disputes a decision they didn't make.

Do this

Pull last month's Default Decision Log. The number of defaulted exceptions tells you exactly which business clients need a training refresh.

Where the board-defensible numbers live.

Two questions come up constantly:

  1. "How many exceptions did we have last month, and what did we do with them?"
  2. "What fraud did we actually catch?"

Both answers live on the dashboard's check-only and ACH-only views, broken down by reason and return reason.

The unlock: decision file delivery and automation.

Here's the part most FIs don't realize is on the table.

Once decisions are made on exceptions, the system generates a decision file the FI's core consumes. Most FIs assume the only option is to log in and download it. That's not the case.

Modern Positive Pay platforms can deliver the decision file anywhere your core can consume it: SFTP to a path the core polls, a pickup queue, even an API call or webhook directly into the core if it supports them.

This is the difference between Positive Pay as "another portal we have to log into" and Positive Pay as "a service running in the background." It's also the conversation starter for broader core automation — increasingly where treasury programs are headed.

"We didn't know we could send the file straight to our core via SFTP. Once we turned it on, our ops team stopped touching the portal. That's when adoption actually stuck."
